An approximate Tutte-decomposition for all connectivities I: Capturing cyclic structure with flowers
Every connected graph trivially decomposes along its cutvertices into 2-connected components. A classical theorem of Tutte from 1966 states that 2-connected graphs can be further decomposed into basic pieces that are cycles, and 3-connected pieces. For higher connectivity, no such decomposition was known until recently, when Carmesin and Kurkofka established a decomposition for 3-connected graphs in 2023. Interestingly, their basic pieces are wheels, which again have a cyclic structure. A recent extension of the Tutte-decomposition to 4-connected graphs by Kurkofka and Planken again produces basic pieces that exhibit a cyclic structure. As it is an open problem to extend the Tutte-decomposition to all connectivities, the crucial question arises whether we can expect the basic pieces of these decompositions to exhibit a cyclic structure?

In this talk, we show that the answer is yes, and thereby make the first step towards a Tutte-decomposition for all connectivities. That is, we use coverings from topology to detect the cyclic structure of the basic building blocks for arbitrary connectivities, and then use flowers from connectivity theory to combinatorially capture those cyclic structures. We have used the flower-description of the basic building blocks to prove an approximate Tutte-decomposition for all connectivities, see the next talk.

This is joint work with Johannes Carmesin, Joseph Devine, Jan Kurkofka and Tim Planken.

Extending isomorphisms of subgraphs: interactions of model theory and combinatorics
Given a graph G (or more generally, a relational structure such as a digraph or hypergraph), a central question in model theory is under which circumstances isomorphisms of induced substructures of G can be extended to automorphisms of G, or how G can be extended to a larger structure in which such an automorphism exists. Perhaps surprisingly, such extensions often yield highly symmetric graphs, and algebraic methods can thus be used to understand and construct them. In my talk, I will give an overview of existing results and open problems in this area.

Separating edges by linearly many subdivisions
Let G be a graph on n vertices. Given two edges e and f in G, we say that a path P in G separates e from f if P contains e but not f. It is known that in every graph G there exists a family of only O(n) paths that together separate all edges of G from each other. Can the same bound be achieved using, instead of paths, another class of subgraphs? In this talk we answer this question affirmatively for families consisting of subdivisions of any fixed arbitrary graph H (and single edges).

Joint work with Matias Pavez-Signe, Maya Stein, Taruni, and Ana Trujillo-Negrete.

Jan Kurkofka
A Tutte-type canonical decomposition of 3- and 4-connected graphs
We provide a unique decomposition of every 4-connected graph into parts that are either quasi-5-connected, cycles of triangle-torsos and 3-connected torsos on at most five vertices, generalised double-wheels, or thickened K4,m's. The decomposition can be described in terms of a tree-decomposition but with edges allowed in the adhesion-sets. Our construction is explicit, canonical, and exhibits a defining property of the Tutte-decomposition.

As a corollary, we obtain a new Tutte-type canonical decomposition of 3-connected graphs into parts that are either quasi-4-connected, generalised wheels, or thickened K3,m's. As an application, we classify all vertex-transitive finite connected graphs as essentially 5-connected or on a short explicit list of graphs.

This is joint work with Tim Planken. The paper is available online: arXiv:2504.00760.

On a refinement of the list chromatic index
The list chromatic index of any graph G is at least its maximum degree Delta(G), since edges incident to any vertex must receive distinct colors. This is most evident when the lists are identical. However, it is reasonable to expect that G is edge-colorable from lists of size less than Delta(G), provided that the lists do not much "locally interfere". There are several ways to make it precise. One approach, which we will consider in our talk, is to bound the maximum number of edges incident to any vertex whose lists have non-empty intersection, introduced by L. Paterek and R. Kang in 2025.

Given a graph G and a natural number d, the ultimate problem is to determine the smallest number g(d) such that G is edge-colorable from any edge-list assignment satisfying that (i) all lists have size at least g(d) and (ii) the maximum number of edges incident to any vertex whose lists have non-empty intersection is at most d. Trivially, g(d) >= d. Our main result shows that, for a simple graph G, we have g(d) = (1 + o(1))d, where o(1) is a term that goes to zero as d goes to infinity. This partially generalizes the celebrated result of J. Kahn, which states that the chromatic index and the list chromatic index of multigraphs are asymptotically equal. Our proof is based on the simpler proof of Kahn's result for simple graphs by M. Molloy and B. Reed.

In our talk, we briefly discuss the proof of our main result. However, main focus will be on presenting several new related open problems and possible algorithmic applications of our results. This is joint work with Ross Kang.

William Joseph Turner
A graph minors approach to temporal sequences
A temporal sequence is a sequence of graphs, each of which is obtained from the previous one via minor operations or the reversal of such operations. We develop a structural approach to simultaneous planarity in temporal sequences, inspired by graph minor theory. Our main result is a classification theorem for 2-connected temporal sequences: we identify five obstruction classes and show that every 2-connected temporal sequence is either simultaneously embeddable or admits a sequence of improvements leading to an obstruction. This structural insight leads to a polynomial-time algorithm for deciding the simultaneous embeddability of 2-connected temporal sequences. The restriction to 2-connected sequences is necessary, as the problem is NP-hard for connected graphs but trivial for 3-connected graphs.

Our results uncover a rich structural theory of temporal planarity, laying the groundwork for a temporal graph minors theory.
